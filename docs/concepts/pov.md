# Point of View

The lens through which the story is shown. There's a lot baked into the lens.

POV operates at more than one level simultaneously:
- Author → Narrator → Character

## Narrative Timeline (POV-dependent)
- Can skip around
- Can contradict
- Can falsify
- 100% of the narrative is present in the work

## History Timeline (POV-independent)
- "Objective" in principle, technically unfalsifiable
- Doesn't skip around
- Contradictions can be recorded but not resolved
- Mostly unknown

## The Vern Problem

Consider Ernest P. Worrell: the audience of those commercials is entirely from Vern's point of view. Vern's reactions are ours by proxy. Vern himself is endlessly patient and forgiving — and our collective viewpoint, having its own agency, chooses to react in a certain way to Ernest's antics. This puts a moral grounding into the narrative of a Braum's commercial without being explicit about it.

The point: the POV character's disposition shapes the audience's moral relationship to the events, without the text stating it. This is worth modeling.

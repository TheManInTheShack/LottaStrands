## Everything is Data

Anything can be analyzed. Everything is data. The principle: if you set up the right observation conditions, even a chaotic or messy thing can be made countable, semi-structured, workable.

Methodology is constrained by equipment, time, labor, and scope. Keep that in mind.

## Structure

Everything has some notion of structure, even if it's just equations governing chaotic motion. The trick is picking a **viewpoint** — a lens — which includes:
- The **context** in which we are observing
- The **resolution** to which we want to focus

### Resolution as a constraint

Think of a Conway's Game of Life grid a trillion cells on a side, viewed at 4k. You could never derive the eight simple rules from that resolution. You'd have to zoom in enormously before individual cell behavior became visible. The point: **the tool shapes what you can see**. Equipment, assumptions, viewpoint, scope, labor, validation all matter before you even get to the analysis.

## Text as Semi-Structured Data

Natural text is semi-structured: the granular pieces (letters/symbols) are already known things. The computer already understands them. The combinations of those pieces — words, phrases, sentences — are where the work is.

- **Words** — buckets of meaning; dictionaries give us programmatic access to their property lists
- **Phrases/sentences** — combinations of words, more constrained but still open-ended
- **Exceptions** — a bucket unto itself; not everything fits the grammar

## Lexicon, Ontology, Taxonomy

**Casting LOTs** — building up a tangled hierarchy of meaning from basic pieces up through what we might call "notions":

- **[[lexicon]]** — the words themselves
- **[[ontology]]** — the basic set of things that can be represented
- **[[taxonomy]]** — the bucket system those things exist in

The result is NOT a cladistic tree but a looser graph where things connect in multiple directions. This is how we establish resolution and how we can view the text from different angles. See also [[semiotics]].
